The Grammar of Refusal

How the Government of India answers Parliament about libraries, when it does not want to answer — and who, exactly, is doing the asking.

In July 2024, M.S. Jothimani, Lok Sabha MP from Karur in Tamil Nadu, filed Unstarred Question No. 1142 to the Minister of Culture. She asked six things, labelled (a) through (f). The details of funds allocated and spent on libraries from 2019 to 2024, state-wise. The new libraries set up in those five years, especially in Tamil Nadu. Whether the Government had a plan to establish a library in Karur. The numbers and list of libraries functioning, state-wise. The libraries upgraded with digital infrastructure. Whether the Government had taken any initiative to bridge the digital- literacy gap in rural India.

It is a serious question. It is a constituency-grounded question. It is exactly the kind of question parliamentary Question Hour exists to answer.

Here is what the Minister of Culture and Tourism, Shri Gajendra Singh Shekhawat, said in reply.

“(a) As per the Seventh schedule of Constitution of India, Library is a State subject and public libraries function under the administrative control of the respective State / Union Territory Authorities. Hence, setting up of Libraries does not come under the purview of National Mission on Libraries, Ministry of Culture.

However, Raja Rammohun Roy Library Foundation (RRRLF), an Autonomous Body under Ministry of Culture, through its matching and non-matching schemes provides financial assistance to public Libraries for the purpose of their development. […]

(b) Reply as under (a) above please. (c) No, Sir. (d) The subject does not fall under Ministry of Culture. (e) Reply as under (a) above please. (f) The subject does not fall under Ministry of Culture.”

That is the verbatim record. This was not a missed deadline, an off-day, a junior officer’s draft. This was the considered, signed reply of a Cabinet Minister to an elected MP, on the floor of the Lok Sabha, about whether the Indian state has a plan to put a library in Karur.

The reply does six things, and none of them is to answer the six things that were asked. It defers (a) to Schedule VII. It points (b) and (e) back to (a). It declines (c) without elaboration. It tells (d) and (f) — the state-wise listings, the digital-literacy initiatives — that the question is addressed to the wrong ministry.

What is striking about this reply is not its evasion. Evasion is universal, older than democracy, baked into the relation between governing power and people who ask it for things. What is striking is the grammar. What is striking is that this reply, and replies like it, recur with such regularity across seventy-five years of Lok Sabha records that they constitute a recognisable register — a way of speaking the State has settled into when the question would, if engaged, demand more than the State has been built to deliver.

This essay is built from three datasets, all public, all re-checkable. We crawled the elibrary.sansad.in archive for every Lok Sabha question that mentions libraries: 4,349 records spanning 1952–2026. We pulled and parsed the verbatim PDFs of 1,485 of them — every record from 1991 onward for which sansad.in publishes a PDF — and applied a regex taxonomy of eight deflection patterns to the answer text. And we cross-referenced the Trivedi Centre for Political Data’s Question Hour dataset (Lok Sabhas 13–16, 2000–2019), which carries asker-level metadata — party, state, constituency, gender — for 1,723 library- relevant rows.

Two headlines, in order of consequence:

1. Across the full 1991–2025 corpus, 42.2% of Lok Sabha library answers — 626 of 1,485 — carry at least one deflection signature. Four percent carry three or more in a single answer. The Karur reply (July 2024) is one of those four percent. It carries four.

2. Modi-era Lok Sabha library replies deflect at nearly twice the rate of the Manmohan Singh era. Deflection rate by Prime Minister, 1991–2025, on full-text PDF answers (regex applied identically): Coalition (1990s) 31.1%; Vajpayee 39.0%; Manmohan 28.4%; Modi 52.6%. The Manmohan window (28%) was the post-liberalisation trough; the Modi window (53%) is the peak — 24 percentage points higher, and 1.85× the Manmohan rate. Same Lok Sabha, same Question Hour mechanism, same set of ministries, same eight regex patterns applied identically across full-text PDFs in each era.

Eight moves

We identified eight recurrent rhetorical signatures of deflection. Each is detectable by simple regex over the verbatim answer text — meaning the finding is reproducible bit-for-bit, not interpretive.

1. Answer-by-pointer — “Reply as under (a) above.” The most common pattern in the corpus, found in 19% of answers. A multi-part question is collapsed by writing one minimal reply and pointing every other sub-part back to it. One evasion answers six.

2. Process gerund — “Steps are being taken.” 14%. Passive present-progressive without an actor, without a deadline, without a named step.

3. Federalism deflection — “It’s a State subject.” Found in 9% of answers. The Centre invokes Schedule VII to frame a cooperative-federal commitment as an exclusive-state burden. The schedule permits state primacy; the practice — RRRLF since 1972, NML since 2014 — is constitutionally cooperative. The deflection treats the schedule as authority not to act.

4. Time-buy — “From time to time.” 8%. Refuses to commit to a cadence; hides the absence of any regular schedule.

5. Scheme invocation — “Under the National Mission on Libraries.” 6%. The scheme name substitutes for the answer: asked about the Centre’s allocation to this state, the answer cites the scheme’s existence.

6. Vagueness on amount — “Adequate funds.” 3%. The questioner asks for a number; the answer hides numbers behind an evaluative adjective.

7. Encourage-without-act — “States are encouraged.” 2%. The Centre dispenses guidance, advisory, exhortation, in lieu of policy.

8. No-data — “Such information is not centrally maintained.” 1%. Comparatively rare in the libraries corpus, but worth naming because it is the move the State reaches for when even the deferral options above no longer apply.

The categories overlap: most flagged answers carry two or more simultaneously. Answer-by-pointer and federalism co-occur in roughly half the records that carry either, because pointers most often point to federalism replies. Federalism and scheme invocation co-occur because the scheme is invoked precisely as the partial exception to the federalism deflection (“library is a State subject; however, RRRLF…”). The grammar nests.

Whom the State refuses to engage

The most consequential finding is not the rate of deflection. It is the asymmetry of it.

We tagged each answer for whether it raised — anywhere in its text — one of eight access-lenses: Dalit / Scheduled Caste; Adivasi / Scheduled Tribe; OBC; minority; women; persons with disability; rural / village; children. Then we counted, per lens, the rate of deflection when that lens was named against the cache-wide baseline rate of 42.2%.

When the answer names… Deflects Vs baseline
OBC / Bahujan 58.7% +16.5 pp
Rural / village 57.3% +15.2 pp
Dalit / SC 54.7% +12.5 pp
Adivasi / ST 53.8% +11.7 pp
Children 53.8% +11.6 pp
Women 52.3% +10.2 pp
Minority 50.7% +8.6 pp
Disability / PwD 46.9% +4.7 pp

The State deflects most when caste, tribe, or rural access is the lens. The OBC / rural / Dalit / Adivasi cluster all sit 12–16 percentage points above baseline. Women and children sit 10–12 pp above. Disability is the lowest of the eight lenses, at +4.7pp — a real but small excess.

The ranking — disability lowest, OBC highest — is a useful diagnostic. The State has, over decades, built named scheme vocabulary for disability-and-libraries: Braille libraries, accessibility provisions inside the broader RRRLF / NML envelopes, specific disbursements that can be cited. When the question names disability, the answer can point at a thing. The grammar of refusal still gets triggered, but less.

For caste, tribe, and rural access, the State has not built the equivalent named-scheme vocabulary. So when the question names these, the answer falls back on what the State does have — the federalism move, the pointer move, the scheme-invocation move applied to schemes that don’t actually address the named gap. The grammar of refusal is the operating procedure for questions about populations the State has not built scheme-language for.

Who is asking?

The lens cross-tab measures who the State refuses to engage. It does not measure who is doing the asking. For that we turn to the TCPD dataset.

Of 1,723 library-relevant Lok Sabha questions filed between 2000 and 2019:

87.9% were filed by men only. 5.4% by women only. 6.7% by mixed-gender co-asker groups. Library access — a topic that disproportionately matters for women, girls, the historically excluded — is overwhelmingly raised in Parliament by men.

The asymmetry is sharper when read next to the lens cross-tab finding. When the answer names women, the State deflects 10 percentage points above baseline. When the question is filed by a woman MP, that already-elevated deflection is layered onto an asker-population that constitutes a fifteenth of all askers. The compounding works in one direction: women-coded questions are filed less often, and answered worse when filed.

By constituency reservation, the picture is closer to parity but still telling: 69% of library questions are filed from General-category constituencies; 10.5% from SC-reserved seats, 7% from ST-reserved seats; about 13% are mixed. SC and ST reservation constitute about 22.5% of Lok Sabha seats, and SC/ST-reserved MPs file about 17.5% of library questions plus their share of the mixed group — close to parity, slightly under. So MPs from reserved seats are not under-asking library questions specifically. But when their answer-text names their constituents — Dalit or Adivasi — the State deflects 12 and 12 percentage points more than baseline. The asker is in the room. The answer steps around them.

By party, the headline number is that BJP MPs file 38% of library questions, INC 19%, with regional parties — Shiv Sena, AIADMK, BJD, CPM, TDP, SP, JD(U) — collectively making up the next ~25%. This number is mostly a function of how many seats each party holds in the relevant Lok Sabhas; it is not, on its own, a finding about which parties care more about libraries. But it is worth noticing that the dominant ruling parties of the period file a large majority of the questions, and that their MPs receive the same proceduralised refusals that opposition MPs do. Constitutional morality, when it has been abandoned, is abandoned across the aisle.

By state, the geographic concentration is striking: Maharashtra accounts for 28% of all library questions in the TCPD subset, Uttar Pradesh 16%, Andhra Pradesh 14%, Tamil Nadu 14%, Bihar 11%. Five states file ~85% of all library questions in the modern record. States with strong public-library traditions raise libraries in Parliament; states without those traditions stay quiet. The question-asking pattern itself is one of the inequalities the project is documenting.

When the State is being watched

The Lok Sabha distinguishes between two question types. Starred questions get an oral answer in the House, with the right to ask supplementary follow-up questions in real time. Unstarred questions get a written reply that is filed on the record but not debated. The two types are filed by the same MPs, on the same topics, in the same sessions. They differ only in whether the Minister has to stand up and say it on the floor.

In our 1,485-PDF cache (142 Starred, 1,342 Unstarred):

77.5% of Starred library answers carry at least one deflection signature. 38.4% of Unstarred answers do. The same Ministry, on the same topic, in the same year, deflects twice as much when the answer is going to be heard live.

This is, as far as we know, the cleanest measurable instance of Habermas’s strategic-action thesis to surface from any Indian parliamentary dataset. When the speech is going to be witnessed, contested, supplementary-questioned, the State performs the form of an answer with maximum compression — gives the briefest, most schedule-citing, most pointer-laden reply it can. When the speech is going to be filed and forgotten, it can afford to be slightly more substantive.

One reading of this is reassuring: the unstarred archives have more useful information than the oral record. The other reading — the one we lean on here — is that the institutional venue most clearly designed for visible accountability has become the venue most clearly designed for visible non-accountability. Question Hour, on libraries, performs evasion in front of the cameras and offers concession in the records nobody reads.

Trajectory across post-liberalisation Prime Ministers

The deflection grammar itself is not new. The federalism move — “library is a State subject” — appears in answers signed by Education Ministers in the 1960s and Culture Ministers in the 2020s. The answer-by-pointer pattern is decades older than any Minister currently in office. The eight rhetorical signatures we identify above can be matched, individually, against replies across the full 1991–2025 window.

What has moved is the frequency at which the grammar gets deployed. Restricting attention to the post-1991 window — the period for which sansad.in publishes per-question PDFs that can be analysed identically — the rate of library replies carrying at least one deflection signature, by Prime Minister:

Era Years Replies w/ full text Deflection rate
Coalition (V.P. Singh / Rao / Deve Gowda / Gujral) 1990–98 344 31.1%
Vajpayee 1998–2004 141 39.0%
Manmohan Singh 2004–14 257 28.4%
Modi 2014–25 743 52.6%

Two visible trajectories. The Manmohan window (28%) was the post-liberalisation trough; the Modi window (53%) is the peak. Over the eleven years from May 2014, library replies became 24 percentage points more likely to carry a deflection signature than they were in the preceding decade. Modi-era replies are also 14pp above the Vajpayee level (39%) and 22pp above the 1990s coalition era (31%).

The four-era pattern is not monotonic but it is informative. Coalition (31%) and Manmohan (28%) sit at the lower end. Vajpayee (39%) and Modi (53%) sit higher. The cleanest reading is that the twenty years from V.P. Singh through Manmohan Singh — across governments of every conceivable political colour — kept the deflection rate in the 28–39% range; the most recent eleven years have moved it significantly above that historical band.

The earlier Lok Sabhas — Nehru, Indira, Rajiv — show even higher deflection rates in our regex pass, but those are computed against question titles only, since sansad.in does not publish per-question PDFs for that period. We do not surface those numbers as findings, because the comparison would not be method-comparable to the post-1991 four-era table. The Coalition / Vajpayee / Manmohan / Modi comparison above is method-comparable: full PDF reply text, identical regex pipeline, identical baseline.

Some surrounding institutional facts shift in parallel. Under Nehru, 40% of library questions were Starred — debated on the floor in oral give-and-take. Under every Prime Minister since, that share has hovered around 10%. Library questions have been moved off the floor of the House, structurally, over six decades. The portfolio also moves: under Manmohan Singh, the Culture Ministry answered 44% of library questions; under Modi, 29% — Education and HRD have reabsorbed the question-load. Neither shift explains the deflection-rate gap. The Modi window’s +24pp deflection rise vs Manmohan is not attributable to the ministry change, the Starred share, or the absolute volume of questions filed — all of which move only modestly in comparison.

The point is not that the abandonment of constitutional morality this dataset documents is a partisan attack. The deflection grammar is the everyday institutional behaviour of the Indian executive across tenures, and we can see the eight signatures in answers signed by Education Ministers under Vajpayee, Culture Ministers under Manmohan, and HRD-era Culture and Education Ministers under Modi. The grammar itself is invariant. What the data does say, unambiguously, is that the frequency with which the grammar gets used to answer library questions has nearly doubled over the last eleven years relative to the decade preceding.

The digital-library question

Roughly a third of the library questions in the TCPD subset (534 of 1,723) are about digital libraries specifically. The yearly distribution is uneven: low through the early 2000s, climbing through the late 2000s, and peaking in 2016 — the year the National Digital Library of India (NDLI) was launched by the Ministry of Education through IIT Kharagpur — and again in 2018.

The digital-library subset is a useful natural experiment in the deflection grammar. Compared to physical libraries, digital libraries fall more clearly under Centre-controlled programmes (NDLI, Digital India). The federalism deflection should be less available, and indeed scheme-invocation crowds it out: questions about NDLI specifically tend to receive answers that name NDLI and cite its existence, rather than pointing back to Schedule VII. This is exactly the pattern we predicted in the disability case, applied to a different kind of named-scheme cover.

But the underlying move is the same. The answer cites the scheme’s existence in lieu of addressing the specific gap the MP raised — the rural reach of the scheme, the state-wise allocation, the actual usage data. The grammar reorganises around the scheme name. The proceduralised refusal continues by other means.

What the theory tells us

Three frames help name what is actually happening.

Habermas: communicative versus strategic action. Communicative action is speech oriented to mutual understanding, accountable to validity-claims about truth, rightness, and sincerity. Strategic action is speech oriented to success, treating the interlocutor instrumentally. Parliament’s Question Hour is constitutionally designed to be communicative — the Executive must hold itself open to the validity of the question. What our 1,485-PDF corpus shows is the obverse: speech-acts that meet the technical obligation to file something on the record while declining to engage the validity-claim of the question. Habermas calls this systematically distorted communication, and his diagnosis of late-modern democracy is that system rationality colonises the lifeworld — that bureaucratic- administrative logic overwrites the register of ordinary citizenship. The bureaucratic “as per Schedule VII” is system-rationality. The asking-MP’s “what about Karur?” is lifeworld. The Starred-vs-Unstarred 77%-vs-38% gap is the visible seam where system overwrites lifeworld in real time, and the gap is widest exactly when accountability is most visible.

Cody: register as the site of power. Francis Cody’s The Light of Knowledge (2013), studying the Pondicherry Arivoli Iyakkam literacy movement, shows that becoming “a political subject the state recognises” is fundamentally a register-acquisition problem. The Indian state has prestigious registers — administrative, bureaucratic, legalistic, schedule-citing — and it recognises questions framed in those registers as answerable. Questions framed in vernacular, person-located, constituency-grounded registers — “what about Karur?” — register as unrefined, demanding to be reformatted before they can be answered. But reformatting strips precisely the political content that motivated the asking. The MP, on behalf of constituents, asks in lifeworld register because that is where the political question lives. The Ministry replies in system register, which is where the political question dies. The deflection isn’t a refusal to answer. It is a refusal to share register — a refusal that is itself a power move.

Ambedkar: constitutional morality vs constitutional text. In the Constituent Assembly debates of 4 November 1948, B.R. Ambedkar drew the distinction between the Constitution — the text — and constitutional morality — the disposition to take the text’s commitments seriously in practice. He warned that without constitutional morality, the Constitution would be a paper instrument. Citing Schedule VII to refuse to answer about library access is constitutional morality’s exact inverse: invoking the Constitution against its own purpose. The text permits state primacy. The morality demands cooperative practice for the populations the Constitution committed to lifting. The State’s deflection grammar is, in Ambedkar’s frame, the everyday, unremarkable, record-filed surrender of constitutional morality to constitutional technicality.

The semioticians (Irvine and Gal, “Language Ideology and Linguistic Differentiation,” 2000) name a related semiotic process: erasure — the systematic rendering invisible of social facts that an ideology cannot accommodate. Each of the seven deflection moves performs erasure. Answer-by-pointer erases the sub-question. Federalism erases the Centre’s role. Scheme invocation erases the gap the scheme failed to close. Process gerund erases the actor and deadline. Vagueness erases the amount. Encouragement erases enforcement. The grammar isn’t decorative. It is the precise linguistic mechanism by which the political content of the question gets transformed into bureaucratic non-content of the answer.

A more honest term than “deflection,” then, would be proceduralised refusal: the refusal is dressed in procedure, which is exactly why it does not read as refusal to a casual reader of the parliamentary record. The procedure is what makes the refusal hard to see. The procedure is the refusal.

This is not a new pattern. Christopher Kelty, writing in Current Anthropology in 2017, traces a “grammar of participation” across twentieth-century institutions — citizen participation in public administration, workplace participation, participatory development — in which the language of inclusion shifts “from a language of normative enthusiasm to one of critiques of co-optation and bureaucratization and back again.” His historical anchor is Selznick’s 1949 study of the Tennessee Valley Authority, where “participation and ‘democracy on the march’ was actually an organizational process of co-opting the farmers, extension workers, and local elites … to the Authority’s projects and goals.” Kelty’s diagnosis of late-twentieth-century participatory development — “a tyranny that had reversed end and means, creating a rigid structure of organizational and documentary demands on projects but that could not demonstrate that participation was actually happening” — applies word-for-word to the Indian Question Hour record on libraries.

We can see this concretely in the Centre’s response to the National Policy on Library and Information System. The Chattopadhyaya Empowered Committee (1986–88) recommended a publicly-funded national library system. The 1993 Working Group, appointed to operationalise the recommendations after the liberalisation budget, recorded almost every item as “ACCEPTED” — and then, in the action column, reduced implementation to inter-departmental letters. Item 1 of the policy — “the most important task is to establish, maintain and strengthen public libraries” — drew this response: “‘Library’ being a State subject, the Department of Culture is not in a position to take direct action.” Item 15, on financing, was ACCEPTED with the implementation note that “the Cell may write to State/UTs and appropriate Central agencies.” The 1993 document reads exactly the way the 2024 Question Hour replies read: each acceptance is accompanied by a procedural manoeuvre that makes the acceptance not bind.

If Kelty’s argument is that “too much democracy in all the wrong places” results from this aspirational-then-bureaucratised cycle, the Indian Question Hour record on libraries shows the same cycle running in reverse: too much procedure in the right place, used to displace the substance of the demand.

What this means politically

Charles Tilly defined democratisation, in Democracy (2007), as increases in the breadth, equality, protection, and mutually-binding consultation between citizens and the state. De-democratisation is the reverse — including, importantly, declines in the equality of consultation: the state consulting some citizens more meaningfully than others.

The Lok Sabha Question Hour is one of the few institutional venues where the equality-of-consultation principle has formal teeth. Every elected MP, regardless of party, region, caste composition of constituency, has the same right to file a question. The Executive is bound to reply on the record. This was never the only mechanism of democratic accountability in India, but it was meant to be a baseline.

Our cross-tabs are, in Tillyan terms, a measurement of de-democratisation as a linguistic differential:

  • The consultation is less mutually-binding exactly when the citizen-population is historically marginalised: +16 percentage points more deflection when the question names OBC constituents, +15 for rural, +13 for Dalit, +12 for Adivasi.
  • The consultation is less mutually-binding exactly when the asking is being publicly observed: 77% deflection on Starred questions vs 38% on Unstarred.
  • The consultation is less symmetrically distributed by gender: 88% of askers are men only, while women-coded questions are 10 percentage points more likely to be deflected.
  • The consultation is less geographically equal: five states file ~85% of all library questions; the bottom twenty file the rest combined.
  • The consultation has intensified over the last decade: deflection was 28% under Manmohan and is 53% under Modi — nearly double — and is the highest of any post-liberalisation Prime Ministerial window.

This is a small, narrow finding about a small, narrow surface: 1,485 Q&A exchanges about libraries in one chamber of one Parliament, plus the 1,723-row TCPD cross-reference. But it is a clean finding. It is not interpretive. It is detectable by regex over verbatim public records that anyone can re-pull and re-check.

And it is consistent with a much larger story that the rest of this project documents through different evidence: that India’s commitment to public libraries — legible across the state Library Acts, the central disbursement records, the international comparisons — is structured along the same axis. The State has not built the schemes; the State has not maintained the funding; the State now does not even answer for the gap on the parliamentary record at the same rate when the gap is asked about for Dalits, Adivasis, rural communities. And the populations with the most at stake in library access are filing the fewest questions about them, in the venues most thoroughly stripped of accountability function.

The political significance of the finding is not “MPs got blown off.” It is that the Indian parliamentary record itself has become a venue of de-democratisation, in the specific sense that its equality-of-consultation principle does not hold for the populations the Constitution most explicitly committed to including. The State files. The State speaks. The grammar is impeccable. What it does not do — at the register the question is asked in, for the populations the question is asked about, in the moments when the answer is going to be heard — is engage.

Methodology and caveats

Three datasets, three windows, three honest limits.

Dataset 1 — sansad.in crawl. Every Lok Sabha question whose metadata or full-text matches one of ~30 library-related search phrases on the elibrary.sansad.in DSpace index. 4,349 metadata records spanning 1952–2026. Used for the per-decade volume, ministry, and Starred-share aggregates on the data page. No party / state / gender / constituency-type information is in this metadata.

Dataset 2 — full-text PDF cache. 1,485 records from Dataset 1, covering essentially every Lok Sabha library question from 1991 onwards for which sansad.in publishes a PDF — i.e., 99.9% coverage of the 1,486 post-1991 records in Dataset 1. (Pre-1991 records, of which there are 2,863 in Dataset 1, are not in PDF form on sansad.in’s DSpace and are excluded from the deflection taxonomy.) pdftotext-extracted answer text. Used for the eight-category deflection taxonomy, the lens cross-tab, and the PM-era deflection rates above.

Dataset 3 — TCPD Question Hour subset. Lok Sabhas 13–16, 2000–2019, filtered to 1,723 library-relevant rows from the Trivedi Centre for Political Data’s 298,294-row Question Hour dump. Used for the asker-demographics analyses (party, state, gender, constituency-type, the digital-library subset).

Limitations:

  • Dataset 2 covers post-1991 essentially in full (99.9% of post-1991 manifest records have a PDF and extracted answer text). Pre-1991 records (Nehru–Rajiv eras) are not in the PDF cache; sansad.in’s DSpace does not publish per-question PDFs for that window. Any pre-1991 deflection figure is regex against question titles only, not method-comparable to the post-1991 four-era table; we intentionally do not surface those numbers as findings here.
  • The TCPD window (Dataset 3) covers 2000–2019. It does not see the most recent five years, including the 2024 Karur reply that opens this essay; the asker-demographic findings should be read as describing the late-Manmohan and Modi-1 era, not the present.
  • Deflection categories are not exclusive. A single answer can carry three or four simultaneously. The 42.2% aggregate rate is records with any signature, not records that are pure deflection from start to finish.
  • The lens cross-tab uses simple keyword detection. Some records that name a lens may do so in a substantive answer rather than a deflection — but the cross-tab is structured to catch only those answers that both name the lens and carry a deflection signature. We are measuring co-occurrence, not causation.
  • Party shares (Dataset 3) are not normalised against MPs-per-party-per-Lok-Sabha. A 38% share for BJP partly reflects parliamentary strength rather than topic preference. The figure should be read as “of the questions filed, X% are filed by an MP from party Y”, not as “party Y disproportionately cares about libraries.”
  • The Rajya Sabha is not yet in scope across any of the three datasets. RS records would roughly double the corpus and may shift the patterns.

The reading

If there is a single takeaway: the parliamentary record about libraries is not where serious accountability for library policy currently lives. It is where serious accountability for library policy appears to live, because the parliamentary record continues to perform every external feature of accountability — questions filed, answers given, signatures applied, transcripts published — while having been hollowed of the internal feature it was meant to perform.

Public libraries in India were built by movements — the Travancore library system under P.N. Panicker; the DMK padippakam programme across Tamil Nadu; the Kerala People’s Plan campaign; the Free Libraries for All project in our own time. They have been refused by the State — refused funding, refused legislation in most states, refused inclusion in the post-2020 NEP digital priorities, and now, on the parliamentary record itself, refused even the ordinary recognition of being a question worth answering at the register the question was asked in.

The grammar of refusal is the State’s everyday, unremarkable, record-filed inheritance from the abandonment of constitutional morality. Documenting it is the small, specific contribution a research-grade dataset can make to the larger argument that this project — and many like it — is making. The data is on the Right to Read site. The verbatim records are on elibrary.sansad.in. The TCPD asker metadata is at lokdhaba.ashoka.edu.in. All three are public. All three are searchable. All three are waiting to be re-read.
