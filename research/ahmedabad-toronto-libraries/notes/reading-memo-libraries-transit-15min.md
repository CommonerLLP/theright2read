# Reading memo — library siting, the 15-minute city, and libraries-as-infrastructure

_Source: Right 2 Read Campaign Zotero corpus (read via partial-recall; no web search).
Item keys are Zotero keys. Compiled 2026-06-14 for the Ahmedabad/Toronto paper._

This memo records what the literature says on three questions raised by the paper's
transit/siting finding, and where each source is woven into the paper.

## 1. Where should a library go? (IFLA/UNESCO siting doctrine)

- **IFLA Public Library Service Guidelines, 2nd rev. ed. (2010), De Gruyter Saur** `[VK7QJXCE]`
  — the authoritative location doctrine: service points belong where people already
  converge; coverage of outlying areas is engineered by *tiering* — central library →
  branch → deposit station → mobile/bookmobile — so that everyone in the area is reached.
- **"Public libraries for Asia: the Delhi Seminar"** `[GBH3RILT]` (UNESCO/IFLA tradition)
  — same doctrine, Asia-specific: the planning unit follows administrative boundaries
  (for India, the district, ~750,000); bookmobiles avoid long gaps and are replaced by a
  branch once demand justifies it.
- **Galvin, "The Small Public Library Building"** `[RK5XPGMU]` — site selection is decisive:
  "the difference between a successful library and a comparative failure can depend upon
  the choice of a site"; favour an open, accessible site near the centre of activity.

→ Paper: cited in the lit-review access paragraph (`@iflaServiceGuidelines2010`). This is
the norm Ahmedabad violates — libraries are not sited where people/transit converge, and
there is no deliberate tiering toward the periphery.

## 2. China's 15-minute city / "community life circle"

- **Yang & Qian (2024), Transactions in Planning and Urban Research** `[UPVVFZWV]` — maps the
  two lineages: Moreno's "15-minute city" (FMC, Paris 2016) vs China's "15-minute community
  life circle" (FLC), planning guidelines **first promulgated in Shanghai, 2016**. A shift
  from "land-oriented" to "human-oriented" planning.
- **Ma, Wang, Li & Sun (2023), Humanities and Social Sciences Communications** `[NCEZPRQJ]` —
  the methodological twin of our paper: a "community life-circle diagnosis system" from urban
  big data scoring facilities on **fairness, accessibility, diversity** across Beijing /
  Shanghai / Shenzhen / Wuhan, and Shanghai longitudinally (2011→2016→2021). Shanghai scores
  highest; the persistent deficit is **the suburbs** — the same periphery problem as Ahmedabad.
- Policy/news context: "Three-Year Action Plan for 15-minute convenient life circles" `[3WVQ3KJW]`;
  `[72MX7K8V]`, `[MZZNYUGE]`, People's Daily `[LQLKVJGT]` (state messaging — use carefully).

→ Paper: cited in the transit section (`@yangQian2024; @maEtal2023`). China is the planned
counter-case — the library is a *mandated* element of the audited walking circle, the opposite
of Ahmedabad's unplanned siting; and the recurring suburban-deficit finding generalises ours.

## 3. Libraries, transit, and the urban — Shannon Mattern especially

Mattern has 15 items in the corpus. Core set:
- **"Library as Infrastructure" (2014), Places Journal** `[3EKXNLUT / CP6XLGK4]` — the anchor:
  the library is social/intellectual/technical **infrastructure**, to be planned and funded
  like other urban systems. (Woven into lit review: `@mattern2014`.)
- **The New Downtown Library: Designing with Communities (2007)** `[HNWCWTLC]` — siting/design
  of a central library *with* its community; most directly on placement.
- **Code and Clay, Data and Dirt (2017)** `[HWPGMJJ6]` — cities have been "smart"/mediated for
  millennia; libraries are urban media infrastructure (anti tech-solutionism).
- **"A City Is Not a Computer" (2017)** `[SF6Y5R6K]` + **"Mission Control: History of the Urban
  Dashboard" (2015)** `[4AFTS83K]` — caution *for our own method*: don't mistake the dashboard/
  map for the city.
- **"Fugitive Libraries" (2019)** `[KC6ESDSH]` + **"Extralibrary Loan" (2025)** `[ER9S4BXG]` —
  libraries pushing beyond their walls to "enlarge the commons" amid a "war on public knowledge"
  — cover for branch/mobile/extension service on corridors rather than one monument.

Adjacent infrastructure scholarship: Anand, Gupta & Appel, *The Promise of Infrastructure* (2018)
`[WVY3RD2T]`; Edwards et al., "An Agenda for Infrastructure Studies" (2009) `[AP2ZI7HQ]`; Cindy Anh
Nguyen, *Bibliotactics* (2026) `[ECVHBYV7]` — the colonial Hanoi/Saigon library as contested public
infrastructure (Asia-specific complement to Mattern).

Transit access specifically:
- **Roy, Bailey & van Noorloos (2024), Journal of Transport Geography** `[L6CVPSX3]` — first/last-mile,
  waiting and transfers, the availability/accessibility/affordability/acceptability frame, Kolkata,
  gendered. (Woven into transit section: `@roy2024`.)
- Verma, Harsha & Subramanian (2021), "Evolution of Urban Transportation Policies in India" `[XNSAD79L]`.
- **Nisar et al. (2018), "Using Algorithms in Resource-Constrained Public Sector: Rural Road Planning
  in India"** `[F79GMHHZ]` — explicitly critiques the *nearest-facility* assumption. (Woven into the
  methods caveat: `@nisar2018`.)

**Gap (honest):** there is no single "transit-and-libraries" paper in the corpus. That junction has to
be *built* — Mattern (library = infrastructure) + the transit-access literature + the 15-min/life-circle
proximity literature. That is exactly the niche the paper's Figure 5 fills.

## Through-line

IFLA gives the **norm** (locate for universal reach, tier toward the periphery, site where people
converge). China gives the **planned counter-case** (the library mandated inside the audited walking
circle). Mattern gives the **theory** (library = civic infrastructure, judged like transit) and a
method-warning (don't mistake the dashboard for the city). All three converge on the paper's finding:
Ahmedabad's libraries are mis-sited relative to where people and transit already are.
