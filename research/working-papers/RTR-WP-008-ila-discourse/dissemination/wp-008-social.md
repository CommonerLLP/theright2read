## 1. SUBSTACK (long-form home, ~1,300 words)

**Title:** The library India costed for sixty-five years and never built
**Subtitle:** How the public library's reader quietly became a "user" — and how the record kept caste unsayable

Begin with the smallest unit of the thing: a person, and a book she has not paid
for. That is what a public library is. For three-quarters of a century the Indian
state worked out, in detail, what it would cost to give her one — and then
declined to pay.

This is not a story of neglect. Neglect forgets. The Indian state *remembered*:
it appointed committee after committee, costed universal free provision down to the
paisa, drafted the bills, and filed them. The library was never lost in a single
act. It was avowed and deferred in the same breath, year upon year, until deferral
set into a refusal no minister ever had to sign.

**The demand was plain, and it was early.** At its 1944 conference the Indian
Library Association asked for one thing: a library guaranteed by central law, paid
from the public purse, free at the door. Central, mandatory, tax-funded, free. The
terms of the obligation were set at the origin — and never met.

**The costing happened, over and over.** In 1939 a Bombay committee weighed a
library cess and declined it, choosing "permissive" provision instead. In 1957 the
Sinha committee called free library access "the right of every citizen … as his
fundamental right," to be funded by a cess of six naye paise — then deferred the
law "gradually within a period of twenty years." The Fourth Plan (1966–71) costed
Rs 30.99 crore and prepared a Draft Bill. None of it was enacted.

What was actually spent? About **2.9 paise per person per year** in 1963–64, and
roughly **three paise — Rs 0.03 — a head** by the early 1980s. Sixty-five years of
arithmetic; almost none of it funded.

> [The figure: "Costed, then declined" — the 1936→2025 timeline.]

**Then the promise was inverted.** In 1993 the obligation was pared to a provision
that libraries "may [charge] Re. 1 to Rs. 10 … as nominal membership fee" — the
very subscription a 1944 committee had called, of a public library, "a
contradiction in terms." By 2025 the answer on the floor of Parliament had reduced
to three sentences: *"The subject 'Libraries' comes under State list. No such data
is maintained. … There is no such plan."*

**While this happened, the reader was renamed.** Read across eighty years of the
profession's own proceedings — nine million words — and one word rises as another
falls: *user* overtakes *reader* in the 1970s; *public library* collapses from its
1950s–60s peak; *digital* surges after 2000. The entitlement became a service; the
citizen became a customer; the commons became a connection you pay to keep.

> [The figure: "The reader becomes a user" — term drift, 1942–2019.]

**And one word almost never appears at all: caste.** Across all forty volumes it
never exceeds 0.45 mentions per 10,000 words. That near-silence is not a gap in the
record. It is the record's deepest finding. The public library was offered as the
caste-free room — but the "public" it imagined never named who it left standing at
the threshold. A free, open-to-all commons is precisely what a caste order has
always resisted.

This is why the same logic that refused the library also meters the tap. The water
Ambedkar marched for at the Chavdar Tank in Mahad in 1927 — the simple right to
drink from a public tank — now reaches Dalit households as a *user charge*: the Jal
Jeevan Mission's own behavioural-design manual lists "Uptake and Payment" as its
first goal. The commons a satyagraha fought to prise open is handed back as a bill.

The right to read cannot be rebuilt in the language that dissolved it. No quantum
of connectivity restores a commons that was never funded. To recover the right to
read is to recover the public library as what 1944 already knew it had to be:
public, tax-funded, in the languages people actually read — and built first for the
readers the commons was designed not to see.
