**Title:** The library India costed for sixty-five years and never built
**Subtitle:** How the public library's reader became a "user" — and how the record kept caste unsayable

Begin with the smallest unit of the thing: a person, and a book she has not paid
for. That is what a public library is. For three-quarters of a century the Indian
state worked out, in detail, what it would cost to give her one — and then declined
to pay.

This is not a story of neglect. Neglect forgets. The Indian state *remembered*. It
appointed committee after committee, costed free provision to the paisa, drafted
the bills, and filed them. The library was never lost in a single act. It was
avowed and deferred in the same breath, year upon year, until deferral hardened
into a refusal no minister ever had to sign.

The demand was plain, and early. At its 1944 conference the Indian Library
Association asked for one thing: a library guaranteed by central law, paid from the
public purse, free at the door. Central, mandatory, tax-funded, free. The terms
were set at the origin — and never met.

The costing kept happening. In 1939 a Bombay committee weighed a library cess and
declined it, choosing "permissive" provision instead. In 1957 the Sinha committee
called free library access "the right of every citizen … as his fundamental right,"
to be funded by a cess of six naye paise — then deferred the law "gradually within
a period of twenty years." The Fourth Plan (1966–71) costed Rs 30.99 crore and
prepared a Draft Bill. After 1986 the deferral even acquired a name to defer — a
National Policy on Library and Information Systems, drafted that year and never laid
before Parliament. None of it was enacted.

What was actually spent? About 2.9 paise per person per year in 1963–64, and
roughly three paise — Rs 0.03 — a head by the early 1980s. Sixty-five years of
arithmetic; almost none of it funded.

Then the promise was inverted. In 1993 the obligation was pared to a provision that
libraries "may [charge] Re. 1 to Rs. 10 … as nominal membership fee" — the very
subscription a 1944 committee had called, of a public library, "a contradiction in
terms." By 2025 the answer on the floor of Parliament had reduced to three
sentences: *"The subject 'Libraries' comes under State list. No such data is
maintained. … There is no such plan."*

While this happened, the reader was renamed. Read across eighty years of the
profession's own proceedings — nine million words — and one word rises as another
falls: *user* overtakes *reader* in the 1970s; *public library* collapses from its
mid-century peak; *digital* surges after 2000. The entitlement became a service;
the citizen became a customer; the commons became a connection you pay to keep.

The timing was not innocent. The break falls on the years around 1991 —
liberalisation, and the collapse of the Soviet book economy that had put cheap
Progress and Mir editions in Indian homes. India's strategic ties with Russia
survived that collapse; the reading commons did not. The relationship was re-based
from books to weapons and barrels.

*[Figure: "The reader becomes a user" — term drift, 1942–2019.]*

And one word almost never appears: caste. Across all forty volumes it never exceeds
0.45 mentions per 10,000 words. That near-silence is not a gap in the record. It is
the record's deepest finding. The public library was offered as the caste-free room
— but the "public" it imagined never named who it left standing at the threshold. A
free, open-to-all commons is precisely what a caste order has always resisted.

This is why the same logic that refused the library now meters the tap. The water
Ambedkar marched for at the Chavdar Tank in Mahad in 1927 — the simple right to
drink from a public tank — reaches Dalit households today as a user charge: the Jal
Jeevan Mission's own behavioural-design manual lists "Uptake and Payment" as its
first goal. The commons a satyagraha fought to prise open is handed back as a bill.

The right to read cannot be rebuilt in the language that dissolved it. No quantum of
connectivity restores a commons that was never funded. To recover the right to read
is to recover the public library as what 1944 already knew it had to be: public,
tax-funded, in the languages people actually read — and built first for the readers
the commons was designed not to see.
